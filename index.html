Timeline for Creation of 3D Printer

  • Tues Sept 5th, 2023

    Our Humble Beginnings

    In the first engineering class at Brandeis University, Introduction to Design Methodology, this is our first project for the creation and assembly of 3D printers.

  • Tues Sept 5th, 2023

    A 3D Printer is Born

    Shown in the "Portfolio" section above is the mini timeline of the creation of our 3D printer, which printed out a 3D boat in the end using a 3D benchy called Thingiverse. In the idealized 3D benchy, it has a single and multi-part STL for 3D printing the boat in one instance. The calibration and settings are as it comes when you open Prusa Slicer. Compared to the printed 3D benchy, it has very similar settings but the scale was lowered down to reduce the print time, which is a very good factor to do for future prints. To improve the calibration of the 3D printer settings, altering the perimeters and layer height to modify the item to print out well in the finish. Doing calibration to a 3D printer allows that specific item to come out the way you wanted it, every time.

  • Tues Sept 5th, 2023

    Transition to Full Capacity

    In between the 3D printer setup, our 3D printer at first did not calibrate our filament correctly causing us to restart multiple times before it worked again. In addition, we used a different print bed, a stable surface for the object to be built on. Most of the other people used the current one on our 3D printer above, but the current one is more efficient in terms of what we needed to be done.

  • Tues Sept 5th, 2023

    Final Level to Expansion

    Finally, after being calibrated multiple times and the extension of assembling the pieces together, the 3D printer was finally finished. It took around 2-3 hours to print our object. It came out very well and gave us the knowledge and ability to now know how to 3D print many more items whilst changing mini components of that specific object.

In this video, is a very well done demonstration from my Professor of how to solder. In one hand, he has a soldering iron and in the other hand is a roll of metal. He put the soldering iron in a bowl of tin to clean off the metal before applying more metal. Shifting Gears

Something really new!

Wow, this engineering course couldn't get any more exciting. Now, we're working on getting a real life client for a topic that we think would benefit people in this world around the area of prosthetics (upper limbs, lower limbs, etc). Out of the many topics discussed and mentioned for choice, my decision was not hard considering I also am a student-athlete, so, I and two of my classmates joined and made a team to create an upper limb prosthesis. Taking Matters into Our Own Hands

Going deeper..

We are now digging deeper into this project to really impact our project and make it a success. In both of these pictures, are an empathy map, one explaining the topic of "Children with upper limb differences participating in sports", and my teammates and I broke it into 4 sections of how our client, the child, feels, says, does or feels when they are participating in their sport with the prosthetic. In the "says" section, we believe our client would maybe say words along the line of, "I want to go play with my friends", or "this prosthetic makes me feel unique and special." In the "does" section, the client would do simple actions such as going to school, hanging out with friends, playing their sport, or just playing outside at recess. Although, some may do the action of hiding their limb difference because they may be ashamed by it, but we are here to make them a prosthetic while also making sure they feel comfortable with it around other people. In the "thinks" section, a common thought would be "I love this sport", but a sad one that is said way too often is, "Why can't I be like other kids?" With this last question being asked, it can be very hard losing a limb due to any sort of accident for example, but comparing yourself to others is not ideal, we are all unique in our own ways, and that will also be displayed to our client and onto others to know they are loved and special the way they are. Now, for the "feels" section, the child could be excited to practice, thankful for this prosthetic so they are able to still do what they love, SPORTS! This is our target goal when we create the prosthetic for them. But also, the child could get frustrated that they can't practice as well like other kids are. This could have them feeling left out, which is not what we want at all. For the second empathy map shown above this description, it is an (x,y) coordinate graph showing the coorelation and benefit for the impact and feasability that this prosthetic has on the child. Some examples are: 1.) Throwing a ball: this would be placed at an impact of (8) and a feasability of (1) because yes you are able to throw the ball, but the chances of it being successful may be slim making it that low. 2.) Quality of interface between wrist and joints: this is at an impact of (7) and feasability of (10) because the impact of this result for wrist and joints is important, but not as important as needing this to be successful for the feasability. Quality interface being the client is able to perform their sport with the best effeciency as possible. Now, we are still waiting to get a client for our project, but we do have another Zoom call with this school teacher named Ryan Dailey, who has actually already involved himself with a little girl who needed a prosthetic for her arm since she played softball, so my teammates and I are going to meet with him on Thursday, November the 9th, 2023 to discuss some questions we have set up and written down to ask him, and so that can point us in the right direction and get us closer to getting a client very soon! Since we have not gathered a client yet, we have not come up with 5 potential ways to solve this problem for our project yet and we do not want to assume 5 potential problems, we would like to get a client, find out what the problem is and know exactly what it is we need to solve to fix that problem of our client. We are definitely on the right track in terms of gathering a client, we just need to keep researching, waiting to hear back from other people, and then expect that this Zoom call with Ryan Dailey will go very well for us, so we can have a client and be able to GET TO WORK! I like the process my teammates and I have come along with so far. We are just getting started for helping these children.

Final Touches.. Plus More to Come

The engineering never ends!

In the first picture, is a picture of my settings in the application IDEA MAKER for the three pieces of the bike handle that was printed in Nylon. The infill was scaled to 90% and the other parameters of the three individual pieces were put at around 75% for perimeter and shape. Now, for the picture above this description, that is a picture of me holding the first iteration we as a team printed out of PLA as a golf arm. This was CAD to CAM for and then PLA. There was one piece of it that did break, but the rest of the pieces shown worked flawlessly. We could incorporate that for one of our sports activities. Overall, from the whole process of testing and changing from CAD to CAM to material change, the biggest difference was using PLA on one of the prints and Nylon on the other. The difference that made us gravitate more towards Nylon is that it was flexible, not too hard, but sturdy enough especially for what we are trying to accomplish. PLA on the other hand, was very easy to snap and/or break, so Nylon was the bigger change we saw between what we usually used as PLA through the engineering course. The prototype I learnt the most from was most likely the third and fourth iteration we printed of the black bike handle and pink arm handle mostly because they came out very close to what we are expecting as our outcome for this project. We are continuing to make this project the best success it can be!
